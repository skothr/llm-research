# Excerpts — Burns et al. 2023, "Weak-to-Strong Generalization" (OpenAI Superalignment)

## Core problem statement {#sec-problem}

> A core challenge for aligning future superhuman AI systems
> (superalignment) is that humans will need to supervise AI systems
> much smarter than them. We study a simple analogy: can small models
> supervise larger ones?

The methodological move is the **analogy substitution**: humans→smaller
model, superhuman AI→larger frontier-base model. The premise is that
the *capability gap* in this analogy is structurally similar to the
*alignment gap* of the actual superalignment problem.

## The setup {#sec-setup}

> We finetune large (GPT-4-family) pretrained models on labels
> generated by small (e.g. GPT-2-level) models, and study how the
> resulting "student" models generalize.

Three task families are studied:

1. **NLP classification tasks** (a curated set of 22 popular NLP
   benchmarks).
2. **Chess puzzles** (move prediction).
3. **ChatGPT reward modeling** (preference prediction).

## The PGR metric — what it measures {#sec-pgr}

The headline metric is **Performance Gap Recovered (PGR)**:

$$\mathrm{PGR} = \frac{\mathrm{strong\_student} - \mathrm{weak\_supervisor}}{\mathrm{strong\_ceiling} - \mathrm{weak\_supervisor}}$$

where strong_ceiling is the strong model finetuned with ground-truth
labels. PGR = 0 means the student matches the supervisor; PGR = 1 means
the student fully recovers the strong-model-with-ground-truth ceiling.

## Headline numerical findings {#sec-numerical}

> Strong models consistently perform better than their weak
> supervisors when naively finetuned … achieving close to GPT-3.5-level
> performance on NLP tasks [with GPT-2-level supervision plus an
> auxiliary confidence loss].

In the abstract framing:
- **Naive finetuning** alone gives a non-trivial PGR but "still leaves
  most of the gap."
- The **auxiliary confidence loss** (penalize the student for low
  confidence on its own predictions) substantially improves PGR on NLP
  tasks.
- On chess puzzles and reward modeling, the auxiliary loss helps less;
  generalization is **task-dependent**.

## Why this matters for scalable oversight {#sec-implications}

The empirical question of the paper is *not* "can the strong student
match the strong ceiling" — that requires ground-truth labels and is
trivially possible. It is "**given only weak supervision, how much of
the strong model's latent capability can we elicit?**" PGR is the
quantitative answer.

[CONTRADICTION] Whether the W2S analogy is a **valid model** of the
actual superalignment setting is contested. Critics (e.g. several 2024
follow-ups) argue that the capability-gap structure between GPT-2 and
GPT-4 is dis-analogous to the human/superhuman gap because both ends
share the same training distribution; the "weak supervisor" still has
seen relevant data, where a human supervising a superhuman model is in
a fundamentally different epistemic position.

## Lineage {#sec-lineage}

W2S sits in the **scalable-oversight** lineage:
- **Debate** (Irving, Christiano, Amodei 2018; arxiv 1805.00899) —
  zero-sum AI vs AI judged by a weaker human/judge.
- **Iterated Amplification (IDA)** (Christiano 2018; alignment forum) —
  recursive decomposition of hard tasks into supervisable subtasks.
- **Constitutional AI / RLAIF** (Bai et al. 2022; arxiv 2212.08073) —
  AI feedback as the supervision signal, governed by a constitution.
- **Weak-to-strong generalization** (this paper) — direct empirical
  study of the gradient itself.
- **Debate with weak LLM judges** (Kenton et al. 2024;
  arxiv 2407.04622) — generalizes Irving 2018's debate to LLM judges
  weaker than the debaters.
