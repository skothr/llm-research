# Excerpts — Muennighoff et al. 2025, "s1: Simple test-time scaling"

## Abstract — verbatim {#abstract}

> Test-time scaling is a promising new approach to language modeling
> that uses extra test-time compute to improve performance. Recently,
> OpenAI's o1 model showed this capability but did not publicly share
> its methodology, leading to many replication efforts. We seek the
> simplest approach to achieve test-time scaling and strong reasoning
> performance. First, we curate a small dataset s1K of 1,000 questions
> paired with reasoning traces relying on three criteria we validate
> through ablations: difficulty, diversity, and quality. Second, we
> develop budget forcing to control test-time compute by forcefully
> terminating the model's thinking process or lengthening it by
> appending "Wait" multiple times to the model's generation when it
> tries to end. This can lead the model to double-check its answer,
> often fixing incorrect reasoning steps. After supervised finetuning
> the Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct language model on s1K and equipping it with
> budget forcing, our model s1-32B exceeds o1-preview on competition
> math questions by up to 27% (MATH and AIME24). Further, scaling
> s1-32B with budget forcing allows extrapolating beyond its
> performance without test-time intervention: from 50% to 57% on
> AIME24. Our model, data, and code are open-source at [github.com/
> simplescaling/s1].

## Headline numbers {#headline}

- **s1K**: 1,000 curated (problem, long-CoT trace, answer) examples.
- **Base model**: Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct.
- Beats o1-preview on competition math by up to **27%** (MATH, AIME24).
- AIME24 scaling under budget forcing: 50% → 57%.
- Three curation criteria: **difficulty, diversity, quality**.

## §2.2 s1K — three filtering stages {#sec-2-2-curation}

The paper describes a three-stage filter from 59K candidates → 1K examples:

1. **Quality** — automated removal of formatting issues (broken LaTeX,
   incomplete reasoning, low-quality scrapes).
2. **Difficulty** — keep examples where reasoning trace length is long and
   model performance (a small reference model) is below threshold;
   difficulty is operationalized as *needs long reasoning to solve*.
3. **Diversity** — partition the remaining set by domain (classified via
   Claude 3.5 Sonnet) and stratified-sample to balance domain coverage.

The 1K-example budget is set so that SFT can run on a single 16-GPU node
in ~7 H100 GPU hours (vs ~394 GPU-hours for the 59K full set; see §5.1
ablation).

## §3.1 Budget forcing — the "Wait" trick {#sec-3-1-budget}

> To enforce a minimum, we suppress the generation of the end-of-thinking
> token delimiter and optionally append the string "Wait" to the model's
> current reasoning trace [to encourage further reflection].

Mechanism (paraphrased from §3.1):

- **Min-budget enforcement.** When the model emits its end-of-thinking
  delimiter before the configured minimum-thinking-budget, mask its
  probability to $-\infty$ and append the literal token "Wait" to the
  trace. The model resumes thinking; this often yields self-correction.
- **Max-budget enforcement.** When the trace exceeds the configured
  maximum, hard-append the end-of-thinking delimiter and force the answer
  phase. Trivial but necessary to control inference cost.

The "Wait" trick is the load-bearing piece — it is what allows test-time
scaling to *extrapolate beyond* the SFT trace lengths.

## §4.2 Headline result — s1-32B vs o1-preview (Table 1) {#sec-4-2-table}

> Table 1 [verbatim numbers, Muennighoff et al. 2025 §4.2]:
>
> | Model         | AIME24 | MATH500 | GPQA Diamond |
> |---------------|--------|---------|--------------|
> | s1-32B (BF)   | 56.7%  | 93.0%   | 59.6%        |
> | o1-preview    | 44.6%  | 85.5%   | 73.3%        |
> | Qwen2.5-32B-I | 26.7%  | 84.0%   | 49.0%        |

s1-32B with budget forcing **exceeds o1-preview on AIME24 by +12.1 pts
and on MATH500 by +7.5 pts**, while remaining below o1-preview on GPQA
Diamond. This is the core paper claim.

## §5.1 Ablation — s1K vs 59K full set {#sec-5-1-ablation}

> The ablation comparing training approaches shows s1K performance at
> 50.0% AIME24 (without budget forcing), while the full 59K dataset
> achieved 53.3% with substantially more compute (394 vs. 7 H100 GPU
> hours).

Two takeaways (paper §5.1):
- The carefully-curated 1K subset achieves ~94% of the 59K-set
  performance at <2% of the SFT compute. The curation criteria
  (difficulty, diversity, quality) are doing most of the work.
- **Budget forcing on top of SFT-on-s1K is what pushes 50% → 56.7% on
  AIME24** — the SFT alone doesn't extrapolate to longer thinking.
